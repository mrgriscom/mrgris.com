--- kind: article created_at: 2026-01-23 title: "Mixing a DJ set in Reaper" --- I am the quintessential "press play" DJ. I don't have the skills to mix live. Nor would I really want to, even if I did have them. If I've done all the work of putting together a DJ set, I want to be enjoying it when it goes on, bouncing on the dancefloor and being not-fully-sober. I _don't_ want to be standing on the sidelines hunched over a computer, filled with the stress of doing a live performance. So for me, the ideal is to mix and pre-record your set in advance. Live DJ software (Rekordbox, Traktor, Serato, Mixxx, etc.) doesn't have a great way to do this. Sure you can record your mix, but that still requires 'performing' it one shot, in realtime, with no chance to fix mistakes or make tweaks later. I don't want to record the outputs; I want to record the inputs ("adjust this slider", "press this button", "nudge this jog wheel")... like the piano roll from a player piano. DJ software isn't built for this. You could probably hack up a way to capture raw MIDI events, but without any track-aware integration that can link events to rhythmic milestones vs. arbitary timestamps, you're just asking for a world of headaches. So, what we need instead is a full DAW. Enter [Reaper](https://www.reaper.fm/). There are many DAWs, but Reaper is fantastic, shockingly affordable (and, without a license, the gentlest of nagware), runs on linux, and is friendly to both the beginner and power-user. Another aspect I'm quite fond of is that its file format is 99% plaintext. That means you can actually examine what changed in your project whenever you worry you accidentally screwed something up. You can even track your project in git! So how do we make a DJ set in Reaper? Well... I'm a rank amaetur in both DJing and digital music production, but I want to share a workflow that was worked well for me, and has been tons of fun. The general principle is: - Load the songs of your playlist into separate DAW tracks - Normalize their playback speed to all have the same BPM (i.e., beatmatch everything) - Arrange the tracks in time for desired mix-in/out points - Use the tracks' volume envelopes to cross-fade - Recover your original desired BPM throughout the set via the playrate envelope on Reaper's "master" track In reality, the full process has a few more steps: - [Track acquisition](#track-dl) - [Track selection and rough cut](#rough-cut) - [_Beatmatching_](#beatmatch) - [_Arranging_](#arranging) - [_Transitions_](#transitions) - [_BPM recovery_](#bpm-recovery) - [Mastering](#mastering) First, a caveat: a DAW is not a DJ controller; trying to replicate every bell and whistle thereof will be a cumbersome and frustrating experience. While Reaper is very powerful, and with enough effort probably _can_ simulate anything a DJ deck can do, that doesn't mean you should. A counter-caveat: **NO ONE CARES ABOUT YOUR DJ TRANSITIONS** Most of my friends DJ using a Spotify playlist with auto-crossfade and everyone still has a great time. Good track selection is 95% of a good mix. Transitions are 5%, and poring over fancy transitions that you aren't even doing live honestly feels a bit cringe. In my opinion, simpler is better (both for the end result and for your sanity), and a DAW does the 'simple' parts very very well. What can a DAW do well vs. not? - _stupid easy_ -- volume crossfade, BPM adjust, pitch adjust, loops, hot cues, samples - _reasonably easy_ -- EQ - _doable_ -- stem separation - _diminishing returns_ -- most FX: echo out, reverb, vinyl effects (scratch, brake, backspin) So why do this at all if you can't showboat your florid transitions? Well, simply, beatmatching is insanely fun. Personally I love the sound of a beatmatched transition; it keeps the energy high and feels like you're being propelled forward into the next track. Now, into the weeds... ## Step 1: Downloading Music MP3s are an endangered species these days, but in order to mix, you need local copies of all your audio files -- streaming services won't do. I can't remember the last time I couldn't find a track I wanted on youtube. With the [yt-dlp](https://github.com/yt-dlp/yt-dlp) command line tool, I can have that track on my hard drive in seconds. The `-x` flag will further extract just the audio. Be sure to install the latest version as these download bots and streaming sites are in a constant arms race. People act like if you download off youtube, your mix will sound so ass you'll have to go into exile. While you will likely be capped at ~128kbps quality and won't win any audiophile awards, in practice it hasn't bothered me. Another nebulous claim is that youtube is EQ'ed/squashed for home listening on crappy laptop speakers, and it will be painfully obvious only once you're on the club sound system. Well, it's hard to falsify that claim because I don't play on the club sound system. But our speakers aren't exactly small and again, it hasn't affected me in practice... There definitely are bad re-rips out there uploaded by gods-knows-who (a risk as old as Napster), but if you get from the official channel, I assume the quality will be sufficient, especially since youtube now runs its own music service too. If you're still concerned, you can buy the track off [Beatport](https://www.beatport.com/). ## Step 2: Track Selection, Rough Cut The earliest stages of making a set involves just messing around with tracks to see what sounds good with each other. For this, I would definitely recommend still getting proficient in a live DJ program (I prefer Mixxx), and doing most of your noodling there. It is software suited to the task, and makes a great playground for experimentation when you're still conceiving your set. Once you have a concept of your track ordering and the transitions you'd like to make, start codifying things in Reaper. ## Step 3: Beatmatching The Reaper beatgrid is our biggest asset. Our goal is to align every track's beats to the beatgrid. Once that is done, we can move tracks around at will while staying... on beat. This is the most technical part of the process, but once you get the hang of it, it can be done quite quickly. The procedure is thus: - load your track into Mixxx (or equivalent) and note its BPM (pic) - create a new track in Reaper and insert the track (Insert > Media file) - right-click on the content > Item properties... - Playback rate > Set... > Source BPM > enter the BPM from Mixxx - turn off 'Loop source' - (optionally, you can transpose the pitch/key here via 'Pitch Adjust') - zoom way in on the first beat of the song (just the first obvious percussion or transient -- doesn't need to be the exact first beat), align this beat to the beat grid via shift+drag (shift disables snapping to grid). My opinion is it's much easier and faster to align the beats visually via the waveform, rather than by ear. (But if you're struggling to align visually, add a click track (Insert > Click Source)) - now examine a beat near the end of the track and ensure it is still aligned to the beat grid - examine 1--3 other places in the interior of the track to ensure beat alignment throughout The following example shows this 'happy path'. (vid of ride or die) If subsequent beats are not aligned: - doublecheck your BPM. EDM will usually have a very consistent and integer BPM - some music indeed has a non-whole-number BPM, or the BPM may waver slightly throughout the track (e.g., vinyl rips, older analog production) - the track truly has a variable tempo/BPM, in which case you have your work cut out for you (see: Advanced Tempo) Make fine adjustments via _stretch markers_. - disable grid snapping (alt+S) - place the cursor on the beat - create a stretch marker (shift+W) - re-enable grid snapping (alt+S) - drag the marker to the beatgrid The beat should now be beatgrid-aligned, and everything in between scaled proportionally. Here's a worked example with Daft Punk's _Harder, Better, Faster, Stronger_. (vid of HBFS) Daft Punk tracks are notorious for unusual BPMs. Let's naively trust the commonly cited BPM of 123 (rounded off from the true BPM of ~123.48). We align the first beat, but due to our imprecise starting BPM, the end of the track needs correction with a stretch marker. Easy enough. The first and last beats are now aligned, but as we scan the track we see the beatgrid is still wrong. The beat goes in and out of phase with the grid twice (a ["beat frequency"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beat_(acoustics)) ha ha), meaning our final beat is 2 beats off. Our beats are slightly too short (since the beat moves opposite to the direction of scan), so we lenghten by moving the stretch marker two beats later. We now need to clean up the start and end of the track due to the nature of the elastic stretch. The stretch is anchored at the start/end of the track, which is not the behavior we want. For the start of the track, we want the stretch anchored at the first _beat_. By instead anchoring at the track start, our first beat that we initially aligned has been pulled slightly out of sync. We can anchor and correct the first beat with another stretch marker. For the end of the track, we don't want it anchored at all. Note how the tail of the track has been squeezed and now plays abnormally fast. We want the tail to play at original speed, i.e., we want the end of the track to 'float' at a fixed offset from the last stretch marker. We do this by deleting the implicit stretch marker placed at the end of the track (alt+click). We might also have to extend the end of the track, as this is not updated automatically. (While the start of track issue is often negligible, end of track is more likely to suffer distorions since the interval between the stretch marker on the final beat and the track end is much much shorter) Note that this example is meant to show what happens when you start which a quite coarse BPM and are off by more than a full beat. Mixxx reports a BPM of 123.5 for this track (and detail view shows you the full unrounded BPM). The more precise your starting BPM, the more likely you can just slightly adjust the final beat via a stretch marker and avoid all these ancillary steps. Here's a track that has a slightly non-constant BPM: (vid self control) It's important to not get too OCD about this. Resist the urge to fully regularize the beatgrid (assuming nothing is grossly off). Humans can rarely resolve beat differences of less than 20 milliseconds (1/25th of a beat at 120 BPM). AND, precise beat alignment only matters when you're mixing with another track. You can always add additional stretch markers later as needed. Get the track intro and outro synced up, don't worry about the middle unless it comes up. (pic before/after) Hopefully, all the track's interior beats have now fallen into place, but if not, use additional stretch markers as needed. If struggling, add a click track We now need to align the track's start to the beat grid, otherwise as soon as we move it, the start will snap to the grid and misalign the beats again. Once your track is fully beat aligned, extend the start of the track so it starts on the beatgrid. Extend until there's 4 bars of lead-in before the first beat. (If extending doesn't prepend the track with silence, you forgot to disable 'Loop source') The 4-bar buffer ensures that, combined with grid snapping, when you move the track around the timeline, it will generally stay musically aligned with other tracks. Note: extending the track start seems to (at least on my version of Reaper) trigger an annoying bug where your beats appear to shift slightly off-grid. As far as I can tell, this is just a artifact of rendering the waveform. If you zoom way in, the beats are still aligned. ## Step 4: Arranging the Timeline, Mix-in/out Points Once this is done for all tracks, you can generally drag your tracks around at will and they won't sound like a trainwreck. More likely than not, they'll even sound good. sometimes a track sneakily extends/reduces a phrase by 1 or 2 bars, breaking the 4/8-bar symmetry thereafter One place where a DAW really shines compared to linear mixing is how easily you can chop up and rearrange tracks. Often there's a track with a good hook, but it drags on for too long or gets repetitive. You can easily cut out just the parts you want. As long as you chop on the 8-bar boundaries, you likely won't even be able to tell something is missing. If there is a noticeable 'jump cut' from e.g., a choral effect, you can create a bit of overlap in the cut and reaper will auto-crossfade the overlapping items. You can also repeat segments of items for looping ripple editing as needed if making changes not at end ## Step 5: DJ Transitions! Volume envelope. How fast the BPM changes is linked to your speed through the track. But… as your BPM increases, you move through the track faster, which means the BPM increases faster, so you move through the track faster… seemingly giving you this runaway effect. I wanted to characterize this.
Math! Let us define our location within the mix as position \\(p\\), in units of beats. Our playback speed is \\( v= \frac{dp}{dt} \\), i.e., \\( \frac{beats}{minute} \\). We have a start BPM \\(v_0\\), a target BPM \\( (v_0 + \Delta v) \\), and an interval of beats over which to complete the transition \\(\Delta p\\). The playrate envelope in reaper will change the BPM \\(v\\) at a fixed slope with respect to position \\(p\\): \\( v(p) = v_0 + \tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta p} p \\) To get BPM with respect to elapsed time \\(t\\) (in minutes), we must solve the differential equation \\( p'(t) = v_0 + \tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta p} p \\) with initial condition \\( p(0) = 0 \\), yielding: $$ p(t) = \frac{v_0}{\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta p}} (e^{\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta p} t}-1) $$ $$ v(t) = v_0 e^{\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta p} t} $$ We can see there is indeed an exponential runaway term. However, this is not how a human DJ would adjust the BPM live. They would move the BPM slider at a steady rate in the time domain, i.e.: \\( v(t) = v_0 + \tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} t \\) This equation is not self-referential, so we can get position via simple integration: \\( p(t) = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} t^2 \\) Note we must solve for \\(\Delta t\\): given \\( p(\Delta t) = \Delta p \\), \\( \Delta t = \frac{\Delta p}{v_0 + \frac{1}{2}\Delta v} \\) To backport this constant-via-time BPM change into the reaper envelope, we must redefine \\( v(t) \\) with respect to \\(p\\). First we must get \\( t(p) \\) from \\( p(t) \\) via the quadratic equation \\( At^2 + Bt + C = 0 \\), where \\( A = \tfrac{1}{2}\tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \\), \\( B = v_0 \\), \\( C = -p \\). $$ t(p) = \frac{-v_0 + \sqrt{v_0^2 + 2\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}p}}{\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}} $$ Substituting \\( t(p) \\) into \\( v(t) \\): $$ \begin{aligned} v(p) &= \sqrt{v_0^2 + 2\tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}p} \\ &= \sqrt{v_0^2 + \frac{2v_0 \Delta v + \Delta v^2}{\Delta p} p} \end{aligned} $$ [ show graph comparing envelopes ] Does this difference matter in practice? [show graph p vs t and v vs t] Apparently not. I'd say it's inconsequential for any sane BPM transition surprised that the relative differences are time invariant We tend to perceive changes as relative difference, so let's analyze how fast the BPM is changing at any moment. What does that look like? We need to compute acceleration \\(a\\) ( \\( \frac{BPM}{minute} \\) i.e., \\( \frac{beats}{min \cdot min} \\) ) with respect to \\(v\\). Reaper method (linear w.r.t. position): \\( a(v) = \tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta p}v \\) (constant relative rate of change to BPM) DJ method (linear w.r.t. time): \\( a = \tfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \\) (constant absolute rate of change to BPM) perceived change will be greater when BPM is lower could actually argue that exponential is more “correct” perceptually
